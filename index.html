About Me

I study how people experience and make sense of digital systems and forms of governance in everyday life, from location-aware mobile platforms to emerging AI technologies.

My doctoral research examined how location-aware smartphones reshape spatial practice and civic identity in Chinese residential communities.

My current work extends into human–AI interaction, focusing on how individuals interpret, trust, and respond to AI-generated content and algorithmic decision-making in everyday platform environments. Across my research, I am interested in how digital systems are not only used, but understood, negotiated, and made meaningful in practice.

I also teach on the MA Digital Media programme at UCL, with a particular focus on research methods and supporting students in developing their own research projects. I am especially interested in making complex theoretical and methodological concepts clear, structured, and applicable to students' own work.

Research

My research explores how people use and experience mobile and digital media in everyday life, drawing out questions of digital governance, identity, and trust as they emerge from the ground up. It has moved from location-based technologies and surveillance during China's pandemic response to how individuals interpret and negotiate AI-generated content and algorithmic decision-making in contemporary platform environments.

Core Module · MA Digital Media: Education · UCL

Digital Media Inquiry

Module Overview

This module introduced postgraduate students to the full arc of research design in digital media contexts, covering audience and textual methods, research ethics (BERA frameworks, GDPR, informed consent), and data collection and analysis. Throughout the term, students engaged with a range of practical tasks that grounded methodological concepts in direct experience.

Two examples include A Place I Call Home, where students created photo essays to explore how researcher positionality shapes data selection, and Lumière Minutes, where students produced one-minute videos of everyday London life as primary data for developing individual research questions.

What I Did

I delivered weekly seminar teaching to a cohort of over 60 postgraduate students, approximately 95% international. Each session combined conceptual input with in-class group discussion, where I provided real-time feedback and guided students through that week's practical tasks.

I taught research ethics through BERA case studies and GDPR frameworks, and led sessions on research question formation, drawing on my own doctoral research to model how a broad topic is narrowed to a precise, answerable question. I also provided regular written feedback on students' weekly work and detailed comments on individual research proposals, identifying conceptual gaps and supporting students in developing methodological precision.

Core Module · MA Digital Media: Education · UCL

Internet Cultures

Module Overview

This module examined how culture is produced, circulated, and contested in networked environments, covering platform dynamics, identity and self-presentation, participatory culture, digital literacies, and AI and algorithmic mediation. Alongside theoretical seminars, students built their own thematic websites and published weekly blog posts responding to the module's themes, developing both a critical framework and a public-facing portfolio over the course of the term.

What I Did

I delivered seminar teaching each week, facilitating discussion that connected theoretical frameworks to platforms and media practices students were already embedded in. Beyond the seminars, I provided regular written feedback and comments on students' weekly blog posts, responding to their arguments, pointing to relevant theory, and helping them develop a clearer analytical voice across the term.

Students chose their own thematic focus for their websites, from horror film as cultural reflection to Korean cinema to travel and urban heritage. My role was to support them in connecting their chosen subject to the module's theoretical concerns, and to give formative feedback that helped each student deepen their analysis week by week.
